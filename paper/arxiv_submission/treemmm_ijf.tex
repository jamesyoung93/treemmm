\begin{abstract}
Marketing mix modeling (MMM) at the healthcare-professional (HCP) panel
scale presents a regime fundamentally different from the aggregate
weekly time-series that motivated classical Bayesian MMM. We present
\TreeMMM, a Python package pairing LightGBM with SHAP for
distribution-aware channel attribution, automatic interaction discovery,
and marginal-ROI optimization on panel data. We benchmark \TreeMMM
against five baselines (GLMM-Naive, GLMM-Oracle, PyMC-Hier-Naive,
PyMC-Hier-Oracle, PyMC-Marketing) on four synthetic datasets. On the
three non-linear datasets, \TreeMMM achieves attribution share-MAPE of
17.9\%~$\pm$~0.2\% vs.\ 22.2\%~$\pm$~0.3\% for GLMM-Naive (gap
4.3~pp~$\pm$~0.4~pp, $N{=}5$ seeds). A distributional-GLM experiment
confirms the advantage is not an artifact of the log$_{1p}$ link
workaround: a properly specified Poisson/Tweedie/Gamma GLM
(GLMMDist-Naive) performs no better, and on two of three datasets
slightly worse. \TreeMMM automatically detects 5 of 6 planted channel
interactions without pre-specification (F1~=~0.56 at default
thresholds); regression-based baselines detect zero. On a geo-panel DGP
designed for PyMC-Marketing's parametric strengths, \TreeMMM
(29.7\%) outperforms PyMC-Marketing (52.1\%) and Meridian (57.0\%). A
prior-variance sweep confirms that at $n{=}3{,}000 \times 36$ periods
all channels move less than 0.1 pp across a 4$\times$ prior-width
bracket. Code and data: \url{https://github.com/jamesyoung93/treemmm}.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} marketing mix modeling, SHAP attribution, gradient
boosted trees, heterogeneous treatment effects, panel data,
interaction discovery, LightGBM

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{The Market Mix Modeling Problem}
\label{sec:problem}

Market Mix Modeling (MMM) seeks to attribute observed commercial
outcomes (sales, prescriptions, conversions) to promotional levers
(advertising, sales force, digital marketing, trade promotions) while
controlling for non-promotional factors (seasonality, competition,
macroeconomics). The stakes are high: MMM outputs directly drive
multi-million-dollar budget allocation decisions across the marketing
portfolio.

Two terms recur throughout this paper and deserve precise definitions
up front. A \emph{Naive} baseline (GLMM-Naive, PyMC-Hier-Naive,
GLMMDist-Naive) uses main effects only: the model knows the list of
promotional and control variables but is given no information about
which pairs interact, what shape each channel's response curve takes,
or which distributional family generated the data. An \emph{Oracle}
baseline (GLMM-Oracle, PyMC-Hier-Oracle, GLMMDist-Oracle) is handed
the exact set of interaction pairs the DGP planted before fitting
(it does \emph{not} receive the interaction coefficients, only which
variable pairs to enter as product terms). The Oracle represents the
upper bound for a regression baseline assuming the analyst already
knows which channels co-modulate; it is unattainable in practice on
real data.

The headline benchmark metric is \emph{attribution-share MAPE}: for
each method we extract per-channel attribution shares (SHAP-derived
for trees, coefficient-derived for regression, posterior-mean-derived
for Bayesian models, all renormalized so promo shares sum to one),
then compute mean absolute percentage error against the DGP
variance-attribution reference defined in \Cref{sec:metrics}. This is
\emph{not} predictive MAPE on the outcome variable (reported separately
as a calibration check in \Cref{sec:pred}).

\subsection{Existing Tools}
\label{sec:tools}

The open-source MMM ecosystem is dominated by Bayesian and ridge
regression approaches. Robyn (Meta) uses ridge regression with
Nevergrad and provides no posterior distributions; Meridian (Google)
implements hierarchical Bayesian MMM via MCMC but assumes parametric
functional forms; PyMC-Marketing (PyMC Labs) is Bayesian via NUTS but
requires the analyst to pre-specify saturation and adstock; Orbit
(Uber) is a Bayesian structural time-series framework not purpose-built
for MMM. Recent academic work has explored neural architectures (NNN,
CausalMMM, DeepCausalMMM \citep{tirumala2025deepcausalmmm}). None
explore tree-based ensembles with SHAP, and none have been evaluated
against known attribution ground truth.

\subsection{The ``Bayesian MMM is superior'' claim is regime-conditional}
\label{sec:regime}

The conventional framing that Bayesian methods are the right default for
marketing mix modeling was forged in a specific data regime: aggregate
weekly time-series with roughly 150 observations, ten or more collinear
channels, and stakeholder demand for posterior intervals. In that regime
the conventional framing is correct. Frequentist ordinary least squares
struggles with weakly identified adstock and saturation parameters,
sign-flipping is a real symptom, and priors regularize estimation while
allowing informed transfer of prior experimental knowledge. These
advantages are real, and they are real because of the data scarcity and
aggregation that define the classical MMM problem.

What changes the calculus is that modern pharmaceutical and B2B
promotional analytics rarely produce data in that regime. Promotional
data at the healthcare-professional (HCP) panel level (10,000 HCPs and
150 weeks gives roughly 1.5 million observations) sits in a different
statistical environment. Four panel-data shifts move the right answer
away from ``default to Bayesian.''

\paragraph{Cross-sectional variation breaks multicollinearity.}
With 1.5 million observations spanning HCPs and time, the rank
deficiency that plagues aggregate weekly MMM disappears. The dominant
identification path is now within-stratum cross-sectional contrast, not
the national-trend separation Bayesian priors are designed to
regularize.

\paragraph{Business constraints bound counterfactuals to in-support regions.}
Minimum and maximum touch constraints per HCP force any budget
reallocation to redistribute over the existing HCP universe rather than
push any individual into unseen territory. Counterfactuals stay within
the convex hull of training support, which is exactly where tree-based
models are reliable.

\paragraph{Composite potential variables absorb observable selection bias.}
Including a well-constructed potential composite (historical
prescribing, panel size, specialty, practice type, payer mix, market
share trends) means that within strata of potential, variation in
promotional intensity approaches as-if-random. Conditioning on this
composite implicitly approximates what double machine learning and
propensity-score methods do formally.

\paragraph{Out-of-sample validation becomes feasible.}
With millions of panel observations, proper time-series cross-validation
produces real out-of-sample prediction guarantees. Aggregate Bayesian
MMM with 150 observations has no honest holdout large enough to validate
response-curve shape.

Under these four shifts, tree-based methods with SHAP attribution are
competitive with or superior to Bayesian MMM for most promotional
decisions. Under the classical aggregate regime, they are not.

\subsection{What \TreeMMM\ is and is not designed for}
\label{sec:scope}

\TreeMMM\ is designed for HCP-level (or store-level, account-level, or
geo-cell-level) panel data with meaningful cross-sectional variation
independent of temporal variation. It is designed for settings where
business constraints bound counterfactuals to in-support regions, where
observable confounders dominate (rich covariates absorb selection bias),
and where the deliverable is response-shape recovery and channel ranking
rather than probabilistic statements about a single scalar. It needs
sample sizes that support proper time-series cross-validation---in
practice at least a few thousand panel-period rows.

\TreeMMM\ is \emph{not} designed for, and this paper does not claim
superiority in, several other regimes.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Aggregate weekly time-series with low observation count}
    ($\approx$150 observations across ten or more channels) is Bayesian
    MMM territory. \TreeMMM\ has fewer degrees of freedom to spare and
    no native adstock or saturation parameterization.

  \item \emph{Brand-new channels} with no historical variation cannot be
    modeled by a tree. A tree cannot estimate the effect of an input it
    has never seen at any non-zero level.

  \item \emph{Formal probabilistic statements} with parameter-level
    credible intervals are outside \TreeMMM's scope. It provides
    prediction intervals via conformalized quantile regression and
    bootstrap, not parametric posterior intervals
    \citep{romano2019cqr}.

  \item \emph{Hierarchical sparse-cell estimation} is a poor fit. Where
    the structure of interest has many small cells (e.g., 200 HCPs by
    50 specialties by 30 payers, with most cells holding fewer than five
    observations), partial pooling via a Bayesian hierarchical model
    dominates.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Decision branches}
\label{sec:decision}

The paper's headline claims live on a concrete branch of the decision
tree. The branch favors Bayesian structural MMM when most of the
following hold: an observation-to-parameter ratio below 20, a
design-matrix condition number above 30, treatment variation primarily
temporal with channels co-moving (correlation above 0.7), credible
informative priors from external evidence, decisions that require
probabilistic statements, and hierarchical units with sparse cells. The
branch favors a tree-based approach (\TreeMMM) when most of the
following hold: panel structure with cross-sectional variation
independent of temporal, treatment overlap reasonable across covariate
strata, counterfactuals that stay within empirical support, confounders
that are largely observable, nonlinearities and interactions that
dominate the response surface, prediction accuracy and out-of-sample
validation as deliverables, and a sample size that supports proper
cross-validation.

The four DGPs in the paper (pharma\_brand, cpg\_brand, saas\_brand,
linear\_baseline) are designed to live on the right-hand branch. The
linear DGP is included as an honesty test: when the data-generating
process is linear and Gaussian, which is the natural home turf of a
GLMM or Bayesian regression, \TreeMMM\ should not dominate. The
benchmark in \Cref{sec:results} confirms this. On the linear DGP,
\TreeMMM\ posts 0.4\%~$\pm$~0.1\% attribution-share MAPE against
GLMM-Naive's 0.3\%~$\pm$~0.0\%.

\subsection{Risks of misuse}
\label{sec:risks}

The paper does not argue that tree-based methods are categorically safer
than Bayesian methods. They have different failure modes.

Bayesian-specific failure modes include tight priors centered on desired
conclusions, identifiability problems masked by smooth posteriors, MCMC
convergence theater, false precision from credible intervals on
misspecified models, and false confidence in extrapolation along
parametric forms. Tree-specific failure modes include confident-looking
SHAP attributions in collinear settings without sensitivity checks, flat
extrapolation outside support, and the temptation to treat prediction
accuracy as causal validity. Shared failure modes include causal claims
from observational data without an identification strategy.

Defensible practice in either paradigm requires showing sensitivity,
support, and identifiability for the recovered effects. \Cref{sec:diagnostics}
documents which of these checks this paper has executed.

\subsection{Diagnostics worth running}
\label{sec:intro_diagnostics}

Five diagnostics determine whether either paradigm is in defensible
territory on a given dataset. All are exposed via the package's
diagnostics API (\texttt{treemmm.core.diagnostics}); the audit of those
executed in this paper appears in \Cref{sec:diagnostics}.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Coverage check}: count training observations within a
    neighborhood of each proposed counterfactual input; under
    $\sim$30 nearest neighbors signals extrapolation regardless of
    method.
  \item \textbf{Identifiability check}: refit with prior variance
    halved/doubled (Bayesian) or seed/hyperparameter perturbations
    (tree). The half-and-double-sigma version is implemented for the
    panel-Bayesian baseline and runs automatically (results in
    \Cref{sec:prior_sensitivity}).
  \item \textbf{Treatment-overlap check}: propensity scores for
    promotional intensity; common support below 80\% across covariate
    strata means no method recovers causal effects without strong
    assumptions.
  \item \textbf{Variation decomposition}: share of total predictor
    variance living within-unit (temporal) vs.\ between-unit
    (cross-sectional).
  \item \textbf{Effective sample size per parameter}:
    \texttt{arviz.ess} / parameter count (Bayesian), training rows /
    leaves at max depth (tree). Below $\sim$20 obs/parameter, both
    paradigms are weakly identified.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Gap and \TreeMMM's Contribution}
\label{sec:gap}

To our knowledge, no pip-installable package, peer-reviewed paper, or
systematic benchmark exists for tree-based MMM with SHAP attribution.
The closest prior art is a 2022 blog post noting ``it is still very
difficult to find examples of SHAP usage in the MMM context'' and a
minimal GitHub repository without SHAP.

\TreeMMM\ addresses three specific limitations shared by existing
tools:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Interaction discovery}. Major MMM tools require
    manually specifying interaction terms. Trees discover interactions
    automatically through split structure.
  \item \textbf{Distribution-aware modeling}. Robyn and Meridian
    default to Gaussian loss. \TreeMMM\ auto-detects the outcome
    distribution and selects the appropriate objective function
    (Poisson, Tweedie, Gamma, Gaussian).
  \item \textbf{Link-function-aware attribution}. SHAP TreeExplainer
    computes values in margin space. For log-link models, naive
    exponentiation of SHAP values violates additivity. \TreeMMM's
    decomposer handles this correctly.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Scope: Synthetic-Only Benchmarks}
\label{sec:scope_synthetic}

All benchmarks reported in this paper are on synthetic
data-generating processes calibrated against pharma, CPG, and SaaS
prior distributions (\Cref{sec:datasets}). Real-world validation
against held-out outcomes, lift-study calibration, or refit stability
on a real panel is deferred to follow-up work. The contribution is
methodological: a panel-MMM tree-based building block (\TreeMMM) with
documented behavior on canonical synthetic regimes, an apples-to-apples
panel-Bayesian comparison that decouples paradigm from aggregation, and
an open-source reproducible package. Regime-applicability guidance is
anchored on the synthetic DGPs and should be revalidated when porting
to a specific brand or panel.

\subsection{Synthetic Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

We evaluate on four synthetic datasets with known ground-truth DGPs
(\Cref{tab:datasets}). All non-linear datasets use heterogeneous
customer sensitivity (HCS), where each customer draws a latent
sensitivity vector from a segment-specific multivariate normal
distribution. Each dataset uses 3,000 entities $\times$ 36 periods.

The pharma DGP (illustrative functional form):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pharma_dgp}
\lambda_{i,t} = \exp\!\Bigl(
  \beta_0
  + \sum_k \beta_{i,k} f_k(x_{i,k,t})
  + \gamma_1 x_{\text{rep},i,t} x_{\text{samples},i,t}
  + \gamma_2 x_{\text{dtc},i,t} x_{\text{rep},i,t}
  + \gamma_3 x_{\text{peer},i,t} x_{\text{rep},i,t}
  + \delta Z_{i,t}
\Bigr),
\quad y_{i,t} \sim \text{NegBin}(\lambda_{i,t},\, r{=}5)
\end{equation}
where $f_k$ is a channel-specific response function (log, sqrt, or
linear), $\beta_{i,k}$ is drawn from a segment-specific multivariate
normal (heterogeneous customer sensitivity), and $\gamma$ controls
interaction strength.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

The evaluation has two distinct axes that should not be conflated.
\emph{Predictive accuracy} asks how close outcome predictions are to
held-out values, summarized by R$^2$ and weighted MAPE on
response-scale predictions. \emph{Attribution recovery} asks whether
the model's decomposition of outcomes onto channels matches the
true DGP, summarized by:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Attribution share-MAPE}: mean absolute percentage error
    between recovered and reference attribution shares (only for
    variables with reference share $>$ 0.5\%);
  \item \textbf{Rank correlation}: Spearman $\rho$ between recovered and
    true attribution rankings;
  \item \textbf{Interaction detection}: whether both variables in a
    planted interaction exceed 3\% SHAP importance;
  \item \textbf{HCS recovery}: Spearman $\rho$ between true latent
    customer sensitivity and customer-level mean $|\phi|$;
  \item \textbf{Distribution matching}: whether the correctly matched
    objective outperforms the mismatched objective.
\end{enumerate}

The headline metric in \Cref{sec:results} is attribution share-MAPE,
not predictive MAPE on responses. Reference attribution shares are
computed as the L$_1$-norm of mean-centered DGP component
contributions, normalized to sum to one. This is a
variance-attribution heuristic, not the Shapley decomposition of the
DGP function; a Monte Carlo Shapley ground truth is planned for a
future release.

\subsection{Benchmark Configuration}
\label{sec:config}

\TreeMMM\ is trained with LightGBM using 20 Optuna hyperparameter
trials per fold, searching over learning rate, number of leaves,
minimum child samples, L1/L2 regularization, and feature/bagging
fractions. Maximum tree depth is constrained to $[3,5]$ and monotone
constraints are enabled for all promotional variables (non-negative
effect direction). Attribution MAPE is computed by comparing L$_1$-centered
SHAP-derived channel shares against L$_1$-centered ground-truth shares,
both on the margin (log/link) scale, which is methodologically
consistent with SHAP's native decomposition on the model's link
function.

\noindent\textbf{Key finding (\Cref{tab:attr_dist}).} The properly
specified distributional GLM (GLMMDist-Naive) does \emph{not} close the

GLMM-Naive to 6.9 pp over GLMMDist-Naive. The bottleneck is the
inability to discover interactions without pre-specification, not the
link function.

Key observations:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Consistent advantage on non-linear DGPs, confirmed robust
    to baseline specification.} \TreeMMM\ beats GLMM-Naive,
    GLMMDist-Naive, and PyMC-Hier-Naive on all three non-linear datasets.
    The distributional-GLM experiment (\Cref{tab:attr_dist}) confirms
    the advantage is not an artifact of the log$_{1p}$ link workaround.

  \item \textbf{GLMMDist-Oracle near-matches \TreeMMM.} With planted
    interactions, the distributional GLM (18.7\%) nearly matches
    \TreeMMM\ (18.3\%), confirming that the value of \TreeMMM\ is
    precisely achieving this without requiring pre-specification.

  \item \textbf{PyMC-Marketing illustrates the aggregation penalty.}
    PyMC-Marketing achieves 75.1\% non-linear average MAPE when
    operating on 36 aggregate rows. The PyMC-Hier-Naive comparison
    shows this is an \emph{aggregation} effect, not a methodological
    one: at fixed panel structure, the same NUTS sampler lands at
    GLMM-Naive parity (gap $\leq$ 0.5 pp on every dataset).

  \item \textbf{Linear honesty.} On the linear DGP, \TreeMMM\ (0.4\%)
    and GLMM (0.3\%) achieve near-perfect attribution. \TreeMMM\ does
    not invent nonlinearity where none exists.
\end{itemize}

\section{Oracle vs.\ Na\"{\i}ve Investigation}
\label{sec:oracle}

\subsection{The Finding}
\label{sec:oracle_finding}

GLMM-Oracle (correctly specified interactions) and its Bayesian
counterpart systematically lose to their Na\"{\i}ve (main-effects-only)
counterparts on attribution share-MAPE at moderate benchmark scales.
Across five seeds, GLMM-Naive averages 24.7\%, GLMM-Oracle 26.2\%. This
is counterintuitive: the Oracle has access to the true DGP.

\subsection{Mechanism}
\label{sec:oracle_mechanism}

The gap is a finite-sample bias--variance trade-off under the 50/50
SHAP-split convention used to define ground-truth interaction
attribution. With three ground-truth interactions all involving
\texttt{rep\_visits}, the Oracle estimates three extra coefficients
beyond the Na\"{\i}ve model, each adding estimation variance that
propagates through the attribution decomposition. The Naive model
partitions interaction effects implicitly into main-effect coefficients
via OLS projection, which has lower variance per share estimate.

The gap closes with sample size and reverses at scale. The
Oracle-minus-Naive gap (in pp of attribution MAPE) is +2.4 at $n{=}50$,
+9.2 at $n{=}100$, +4.4 at $n{=}200$, +3.5 at $n{=}500$, and $-$2.8
at $n{=}1{,}000$. The Oracle's asymptotic-bias advantage eventually
dominates its finite-sample variance penalty, but only at sample sizes
well past the default benchmark.

\subsection{Implication for the Benchmark}
\label{sec:oracle_implication}

This is not a metric pathology. It is a property of the comparison scale
chosen. Reports of ``Oracle wins'' should use $n \geq 1{,}000$ and note
explicitly that the comparison is in the asymptotic regime.

\section{Diagnostics Framework}
\label{sec:diagnostics}

Three of the five regime diagnostics described in \Cref{sec:intro}
are now runnable as one-line calls.

\paragraph{Coverage check.} \texttt{treemmm.core.diagnostics.regime\_check.coverage\_check(X\_train, X\_simulated, radius, min\_neighbors)} counts training observations within a standardized-Euclidean radius of each counterfactual input. The default rule treats a simulated point as covered when at least 30 training neighbors fall inside half a standard deviation, passing when at least 80\% of simulated points clear that bar.

\paragraph{Variation decomposition.} \texttt{variation\_decomposition(df, unit\_col, feature\_cols)} reports each predictor's variance split into within-unit (temporal) and between-unit (cross-sectional) components.

\paragraph{Tree effective sample size.} \texttt{tree\_ess\_per\_param(n\_train, n\_estimators, max\_depth)} returns the ratio of training rows to an upper bound on the number of ensemble leaves, with a rule of thumb of at least 20 effective observations per parameter.

\paragraph{Bayesian prior sensitivity.} Fully wired into \texttt{paper/run\_benchmarks.py}; emits \texttt{paper/results/prior\_sensitivity.csv} plus \Cref{fig:prior_sensitivity} by default (see \Cref{sec:prior_sensitivity}).

Two diagnostics remain follow-up work: treatment-overlap propensity-score checks and formal SHAP-stability-under-collinearity audits (\Cref{sec:followup}).

\section{Limitations and Follow-Up Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\subsection{Known Limitations}
\label{sec:known_limits}

All results come from synthetic benchmarks with a small number of seeds.
Real-world validation has not been carried out. Both GLMM configurations
use a log-transform workaround; a properly specified distributional GLMM
(e.g., \texttt{glmmTMB} in R) would be a stronger baseline, and Table~2b
suggests that closing the link-function gap would not eliminate
\TreeMMM's advantage. Attribution shares are compared against a
variance-attribution heuristic (L$_1$-centered component contributions),
not the Shapley decomposition of the DGP function; a Monte Carlo Shapley
ground truth is planned. Sensitivity to LightGBM hyperparameter choices
(number of Optuna trials, depth range, monotone constraints) is not
explored and is a limitation.

\subsection{Follow-Up Tasks}
\label{sec:followup}

\begin{enumerate}

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Distribution-Aware Objective Selection}
\label{sec:objective}

\TreeMMM\ supports four objectives matched to outcome distributions:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Supported objectives and their use cases.}
\label{tab:objectives}
\begin{tabular}{llp{6cm}}
\toprule
Distribution & Objective & When to Use \\
\midrule
Gaussian  & MSE       & Continuous, symmetric (revenue, value sales) \\
Poisson   & Log-link  & Non-negative counts (Rx, orders, NPS) \\
Tweedie   & Log-link  & Zero-inflated continuous (revenue with stockouts) \\
Gamma     & Log-link  & Strictly positive continuous (per-transaction revenue) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

An automated diagnostic examines discreteness, zero-inflation rate,
skewness, and the mean-variance relationship to recommend an objective.
The user can override this recommendation.

\subsection{Link-Function-Aware Attribution Decomposition}
\label{sec:decomposition}

SHAP TreeExplainer computes values in margin space. For identity-link
(Gaussian) models, SHAP values are directly additive on the response
scale. For log-link (Poisson/Tweedie/Gamma) models, naive
exponentiation breaks additivity: $\exp(a + b) \neq \exp(a) + \exp(b)$.
\TreeMMM's decomposer uses unsigned proportional allocation:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:attribution}
  \hat{a}_i = \frac{|\phi_i|}{|\phi_0| + \sum_j |\phi_j|} \cdot \hat{y}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_i$ is the SHAP value for feature $i$, $\phi_0$ is the base
value, and $\hat{y}$ is the model prediction. This guarantees (1)
non-negative attributions (standard MMM practice), (2) attributions sum
to the predicted outcome for every observation, and (3) the relative
ranking of channels is preserved from the log-space SHAP values.

\subsection{Temporal Cross-Validation}
\label{sec:cv}

\TreeMMM\ uses time-respecting validation to prevent future data
leakage: rolling-origin training windows grow forward, and test windows
are always future periods. The minimum training fraction is configurable
(default: 50\% of periods). No observation from a future period ever
appears in the training set.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Tuning}
\label{sec:tuning}

Optuna Bayesian optimization tunes tree hyperparameters within each
cross-validation fold: number of leaves, learning rate, regularization
(L1/L2), feature/bagging fraction, and minimum child samples. The
tuning objective is distribution-matched (Poisson deviance for counts,
Tweedie deviance for zero-inflated outcomes, MSE for Gaussian).

\subsection{Marginal ROI Simulation with Extrapolation Safety}
\label{sec:mroi}

The mROI simulator estimates response curves with a critical safety
constraint: per-customer caps are set at the observed percentile
(default: 95th), and higher aggregate engagement is achieved by
spreading to more customers rather than pushing any individual beyond
observed bounds. The constrained optimizer (scipy SLSQP) surfaces which
customers have untapped marginal sensitivity, revealing that
``see your top customers the most'' is often suboptimal.

\subsection{Baseline Models}
\label{sec:baselines}

We compare \TreeMMM\ against five baselines spanning three modeling
paradigms.

\paragraph{GLMM-Naive.} Main effects only, random intercepts per
customer (statsmodels MixedLM). Represents a typical analyst who does
not specify interactions or distributional families.

\paragraph{GLMM-Oracle.} Correctly specified interaction terms matching
the DGP (statsmodels MixedLM). Represents an analyst with perfect
knowledge of which variables interact.

Both GLMM configurations use a log-transform workaround on non-Gaussian
datasets (log-transform the outcome before fitting MixedLM), which
approximates a log-normal model. This is a common practitioner
workaround. A properly specified distributional GLMM (e.g.,
\texttt{glmmTMB} in R) is not equivalent; see \Cref{sec:limitations}
for discussion.

\paragraph{PyMC-Hier-Naive.} A hierarchical Bayesian linear MMM fit
on the full panel (3,000 entities $\times$ 36 periods, $\approx$108,000
rows), with main effects only and a customer-level random intercept.
Prior structure (after standardization): $\alpha \sim \mathcal{N}(0,5)$,
$a_\text{cust} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\text{cust})$ with
$\sigma_\text{cust} \sim \text{HalfNormal}(2)$,
$\beta_\text{main} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, and
$\sigma_\text{obs} \sim \text{HalfNormal}(1)$. Posterior sampling uses
\texttt{nutpie} (Rust-backed NUTS) with four chains, 1,000 tuning and
1,000 draw steps each.

\paragraph{PyMC-Hier-Oracle.} Identical to PyMC-Hier-Naive but with the
planted DGP interaction terms added as fixed effects
($\beta_\text{inter} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,0.5)$). Forms the Bayesian
counterpart of GLMM-Oracle.

\paragraph{PyMC-Marketing.} The leading open-source Bayesian MMM
\citep{pymcmarketing2025}, implementing NUTS sampling with geometric
adstock and logistic saturation transforms on aggregate time-series (one
row per time period, 36 rows). We use default weakly informative priors,
\texttt{GeometricAdstock(l\_max=4)}, \texttt{LogisticSaturation()}, and
the NumPyro sampler (500 draws, 500 tuning, 2 chains).

that matter for interpreting results.
